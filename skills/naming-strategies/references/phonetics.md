# Phonetic Analysis Criteria

How a name sounds is as important as what it means. These criteria guide phonetic evaluation of name candidates.

## Consonant Types

### Hard Consonants (K, P, T, B, D, G)

Convey **strength, boldness, technology, and authority**.

- Plosive sounds create impact and memorability
- Effective for tech brands, tools, and performance products
- Examples: Kodak, BlackBerry, TikTok, Docker

### Soft Consonants (L, M, N, S, F)

Convey **friendliness, comfort, elegance, and approachability**.

- Continuant sounds feel smooth and inviting
- Effective for wellness, lifestyle, and luxury brands
- Examples: Lululemon, Samsung, Sonos, Calm

### Mixed Patterns

Most strong names combine both — a hard consonant for impact and soft consonants for flow:

- **Google** — hard G bookends, soft oo in the middle
- **Apple** — soft start, hard P for punch, soft L finish
- **Tesla** — hard T start, soft SLA flow

## Vowel Patterns

### Open Vowels (A, O)

Convey **warmth, openness, and accessibility**.

- Open mouth position creates a sense of welcome
- Effective for consumer brands and social products
- Examples: Amazon, Sonos, Yahoo

### Closed Vowels (I, U, E)

Convey **precision, speed, and technical sophistication**.

- Tight mouth position creates a sense of focus
- Effective for tech tools, B2B, and precision products
- Examples: Pinterest, Uber, LinkedIn

### Vowel Harmony

Names where vowels follow a consistent pattern (front-to-back or back-to-front) feel more natural and are easier to remember. Avoid jarring vowel jumps within short names.

## Syllable Count

| Syllables | Characteristics                              | Best For                                      |
| --------- | -------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------- |
| 1         | Punchy, commanding, easy to shout            | Action brands, commands (Slack, Zoom, Bolt)   |
| 2         | Optimal balance of memorability and identity | Most brands (Apple, Google, Facebook)         |
| 3         | Rhythmic, can feel premium or technical      | Premium/corporate (Amazon, Samsung, Mercedes) |
| 4+        | Harder to remember, often shortened          | Avoid unless abbreviation is planned          |

**2-3 syllables is optimal for memorability.** One-syllable names are powerful but hard to trademark. Four or more syllables will be shortened by users whether you plan for it or not.

## The Radio Test

**Can the name be understood when heard, not seen?**

Test by saying the name aloud in a noisy environment. Ask:

- Would someone catch it on a podcast or radio ad?
- Can it be distinguished from similar-sounding words?
- Is the emphasis pattern natural and unambiguous?

**Failures:**

- Names that sound like common words ("Cite" sounds like "site" and "sight")
- Names with ambiguous stress ("reCORD" vs "REcord")
- Names that blend into surrounding speech

## The Spelling Test

**Can someone spell the name correctly after hearing it once?**

This directly impacts word-of-mouth growth and search discoverability.

**Failures:**

- Non-obvious vowel removal (is it Tumblr or Tumbler?)
- Silent letters or unusual letter combinations
- Multiple plausible spellings (Keira? Kira? Kyra?)

**Mitigations:**

- If the name intentionally breaks spelling rules (Lyft, Flickr), ensure the brand can invest in awareness
- Own common misspellings as redirecting domains

## Cross-Language Pronunciation Pitfalls

### Gender Associations

- Names ending in **"-a"** sound feminine in Romance languages (Spanish, Italian, Portuguese)
- Names ending in **"-o"** sound masculine in the same languages
- Names ending in **"-e"** are more neutral across languages

### Missing Sounds

- The **"SH"** sound does not exist in many languages (Spanish, Italian, Arabic)
- The **"TH"** sound (as in "the") is rare outside English and Greek
- The **"R"** sound varies dramatically — French uvular, Spanish trilled, Japanese approximant
- The **"V"** sound does not exist in many East Asian languages

### Nasal Vowels

- French nasal vowels (on, an, in, un) are difficult for non-French speakers
- Avoid building names around sounds that only exist in one language family

### Profanity and Negative Meaning Checks

Before finalising any name, check against common profanity and negative connotations in at least these languages:

- English, Spanish, French, German, Portuguese
- Mandarin, Japanese, Korean
- Arabic, Hindi

Even partial phonetic overlap with offensive words in major languages can sink a brand. Use native speakers or language consultants for final validation.
