# Naming Strategies

7 distinct strategies for generating brand and product names. Each strategy includes when to use it, how to generate names with it, and real-world examples.

## 1. Neologisms

**Invented words with no prior meaning.**

Examples: Kodak, Xerox, Spotify, Rolex, Haagen-Dazs

**When to use:**

- Tech products and innovative brands
- When trademark protection is a priority (no prior usage to contest)
- When you want a blank-slate brand identity with no inherited meaning
- Startups wanting to own their category

**How to generate:**

- Combine morphemes from different languages (e.g., "Spoti" + "fy")
- Use phonetically pleasing consonant-vowel combinations
- Target 2-3 syllables for optimal memorability
- Start with sounds that match the brand personality (hard consonants for tech, soft for wellness)
- Test that the word has no unintended meanings in major languages

## 2. Compound Words

**Two real words merged together.**

Examples: Facebook, YouTube, AirBnB, Snapchat, WordPress, Firefox

**When to use:**

- Descriptive brands where meaning should be immediately obvious
- Consumer products targeting broad audiences
- When the name needs to communicate function or category
- Products where discoverability matters (people search for component words)

**How to generate:**

- Combine a descriptor + category word (e.g., "Face" + "book")
- Merge two abstract concepts that together evoke the brand (e.g., "Fire" + "fox")
- Try noun + noun, adjective + noun, or verb + noun patterns
- Test with and without camelCase — both forms should read naturally
- Keep total syllable count under 4 for spoken ease

## 3. Metaphors/Analogies

**Words borrowed from another domain to evoke feelings or associations.**

Examples: Amazon, Apple, Jaguar, Puma, Shell, Patagonia, North Face

**When to use:**

- Emotional brands and lifestyle products
- When evoking specific feelings matters more than describing the product
- Premium positioning where association carries weight
- Brands that want to tell a story through their name

**How to generate:**

- Identify 3-5 core brand values (speed, reliability, exploration, etc.)
- For each value, list objects, animals, places, or concepts that embody it
- Filter for names that are simple, memorable, and not already dominant brands
- Prefer concrete, visual words over abstract concepts (people remember images)
- Test the metaphor's resonance across cultures — some animals/objects carry different connotations

## 4. Acronyms

**Shortened from key words in a longer name or phrase.**

Examples: IBM (International Business Machines), BMW (Bayerische Motoren Werke), IKEA (Ingvar Kamprad Elmtaryd Agunnaryd), NASA

**When to use:**

- B2B and technical products
- Corporate brands where the full name is too long for everyday use
- When the acronym itself is pronounceable as a word (NASA, IKEA)
- Legacy brands transitioning from formal to modern identity

**How to generate:**

- Start with a descriptive phrase that captures the brand's essence
- Extract initials and test pronounceability as a word vs. spelled out
- Pronounceable acronyms (3-4 letters) are stronger than spelled-out ones
- Avoid acronyms that spell existing words with negative connotations
- Consider backronyms: start with a target word, then reverse-engineer the phrase

## 5. Word Modifications

**Altered real words — vowel removal, letter swaps, suffix changes.**

Examples: Tumblr, Flickr, Lyft, Reddit (read it), Digg, Scribd

**When to use:**

- Playful brands and tech startups
- When an existing word fits perfectly but needs uniqueness for trademarking
- Casual, approachable brand personalities
- When the original word should still be recognizable

**How to generate:**

- Start with a word that describes the product or experience
- Remove vowels (Tumblr, Flickr, Scribd)
- Swap letters for phonetic equivalents (Lyft = Lift, Digg = Dig)
- Add or remove suffixes (-ly, -ify, -er, -io)
- Double a letter for emphasis (Digg, Fiverr)
- Test that the modification doesn't make the word unrecognizable or unpronounceable

## 6. Foreign Language

**Borrowings from other languages, real or fabricated.**

Examples: Audi (Latin: "listen"), Volvo (Latin: "I roll"), Lego (Danish: "play well"), Samsung (Korean: "three stars"), Hulu (Mandarin: "gourd")

**When to use:**

- Premium and luxury brands seeking sophistication
- Cultural positioning for specific markets
- International markets where neutrality across languages matters
- When Latin/Greek roots add authority (medical, legal, academic)

**How to generate:**

- Translate core brand values into Latin, Greek, Japanese, Italian, or other target languages
- Use roots and morphemes rather than full words for originality
- Latin and Greek convey authority and timelessness
- Japanese conveys precision and minimalism
- Italian and French convey style and luxury
- Verify the word doesn't have negative meanings in other major languages
- Consider fabricated foreign-sounding words (Haagen-Dazs is not actually Scandinavian)

## 7. Evocative/Sound Symbolism

**Names that sound like what they represent — the sound itself carries meaning.**

Examples: Zoom, Slack, Crash, Swoosh, Ping, Snap, Bolt

**When to use:**

- Action-oriented brands where the name should convey energy or feeling
- Products where speed, impact, or sensory experience is the selling point
- When the name needs to be viscerally understood, not intellectually parsed
- Short, punchy brand identities

**How to generate:**

- Use onomatopoeia directly (Snap, Crash, Ping, Buzz)
- Use phonesthemes — sound clusters that carry inherent meaning:
  - **gl-** = light, glow (gleam, glitter, glow, glint)
  - **sn-** = nose, quick (snap, sniff, snare, snag)
  - **cr-** = impact, breaking (crash, crack, crunch, crush)
  - **sl-** = smooth, sliding (slide, slick, slip, sleek)
  - **sw-** = sweeping motion (swish, swing, swirl, sweep)
  - **fl-** = movement, light (flash, flow, flutter, flick)
  - **str-** = strength, tension (strong, stretch, strike, stride)
- Match the word's rhythm to the product's energy — short and sharp for speed, flowing for comfort
- Test the name spoken aloud — the sound should feel right before the meaning registers
